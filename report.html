My Report

By Keagan Suah

Introduction: What is the essential story being told by your portfolio site? Describe the version of you that it is about. What is the audience you are addressing? What type of structure did you choose to implement?

The essential story that I am trying to convey with my portfolio website is my experience, projects, and programming journey to my audiences. I have created a portfolio website before to showcase my projects to potential recruiters. But the website that I have created previously is based on a standardized template, and is what most people normally have. With my current portfolio website, I wish to convey that I have a creative side in me to design and animate stuff that is out of the norm, while also maintaining standardization and professionalism.

The content of this project is given to me to create a portfolio website about me in the year 2034. I interpreted this question as a common question asked in the interview question, “Where do I see myself 10 years down the road,” and decided to create a portfolio website based on that. This portfolio website is a version of what I plan to achieve, as well as where I want to be in the future. This means that I will have more skillsets, more hours into programming and projects, and most importantly, more experience working in reputable companies.

The main audience that I wish to address is potential recruiters looking to hire me. As my resume alone makes it hard to tell what I am capable of doing, I wish to use this portfolio website to showcase my ability to create fun and interactive websites, while also being able to showcase my other projects, such as machine learning and data science. I also want to showcase my programming journey, to show what contribution I have made to previous employers. While the main audience is recruiters or employers, I also cater this website to people who are just curious about who I am and what I can do, or people who are just starting programming. I wish to inspire them by showing my ability and past work.

The structure of my site is a linked structure, where one page cross-links to one another. When the audience first visits my website, they will be greeted with my hero section on my home page, which is visually appealing and interactive, as it features a parallax scrolling, just to add some fun to my website. It will then provide a summary about me to indicate why recruiters should choose me, as well as my testimonials. Depending on what the audience clicks, they will be brought to either my portfolio page or the about me page. The About Me page shows my journey, which includes my experience and education. Below are the skills I have learned, such as frameworks or programming languages, and the services that I am capable of.

My portfolio page shows the featured projects I have done, and audiences can click on them to get more information. The information will be presented in a pop-up. Through the nav link, audiences are then able to navigate to my blog page, which shows how I keep updated on technology. If the audience is interested based on what I did, they can contact me through the Contact Me page, which provides all my social media, my resume, my email, or just message me through the website.

I spend a lot of time researching another professional website that offers interactive animations as well as eye-catching designs to attract audiences. I managed to find three websites that I liked the best, as they provide cool animation and unique design.

Accessibility: State 3 ways in which your site is accessible to those with different abilities and needs (for example those with visual impairment)

The first accessibility feature I implemented is a flexible layout and adaptable viewport. To ensure the site is accessible across different devices and screen sizes, I designed it to fluidly adapt to three main viewports: desktop, tablet, and mobile. Instead of relying on fixed pixel sizes, I used flexible CSS units like percentages, vw (viewport width), and vh (viewport height), allowing the site to scale appropriately for different screen sizes. This flexibility is particularly helpful for users who depend on screen magnifiers or use devices with various resolutions.

The second accessibility feature I implemented is enhanced screen reader support through semantic HTML. I applied accessibility best practices, such as including descriptive alt attributes for all images and using appropriate ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications) labels, to ensure key elements are accessible to screen readers. To improve navigability for users with visual impairments, I used semantic HTML elements like header, main, nav, and article, rather than generic div tags. This approach not only makes the content easier for screen readers to interpret but also improves the overall structure, making it simpler for users with disabilities to understand and navigate the site.

The third accessibility feature is keyboard accessibility and focus states. Recognising the needs of users with physical disabilities, I made sure the website is fully navigable using only a keyboard. The "tab" key can be used to move through interactive elements such as links, buttons, and forms, ensuring the site is accessible for those unable to use a mouse. Additionally, I implemented a focus state indicator similar to the hover effect. This provides visual feedback, highlighting the currently selected element when navigating via keyboard, offering a clear visual cue that enhances usability for everyone, particularly those with motor disabilities. The focus state helps maintain a consistent user experience and makes the site more intuitive for keyboard-only users.

Usability: State 3 ways in which you considered the usability of your site.

The first usability feature I focused on is responsive and intuitive navigation with a hamburger menu. I prioritised usability by designing a simple, efficient navigation system that adjusts to different devices. On desktop, users see a traditional top navigation bar, making all sections of the site easily accessible. For smaller viewports, like mobile devices, I implemented a hamburger menu, allowing users to access navigation through a flyout menu. This keeps the interface clutter-free while ensuring ease of use. The transition from a desktop navigation bar to a mobile-friendly hamburger menu is managed with JavaScript, providing a seamless and intuitive experience across all screen sizes. This design ensures users can navigate smoothly, regardless of the device they’re using.

The second usability feature I considered is a high-contrast colour scheme for improved readability. To deliver a user-friendly experience, I carefully selected the colour scheme and contrast throughout the site. I chose three main colours and adjusted their brightness levels to distinguish important content from less important elements. This high-contrast colour palette enhances readability, especially for users with visual impairments or those viewing the site in less-than-ideal lighting conditions. By consistently applying these colours, I was able to create a visually appealing and functional design that balances both usability and accessibility. This ensures that text and key elements are clear and easy to understand, making the site more intuitive for a wide range of users.

The third usability feature I incorporated is light and dark mode for a personalised user experience. Another key feature I implemented is the option for users to toggle between light and dark modes. This choice is easily accessible via a button at the top of the page, enabling users to select their preferred mode based on their environment or comfort. I thoroughly tested both modes across all pages to ensure a consistently positive experience. Additionally, I used localStorage to remember the user’s preferred mode, so they don't have to reset it every time they visit a new page. This provides a more personalised and convenient browsing experience, especially for users who frequently switch between light and dark environments.

State 3 things you had to learn or find out to create your site. How did you achieve that?

The first thing I had to learn was how to implement dynamic content loading. At first, I struggled with understanding how to update content on the fly without reloading the entire page. I explored JavaScript’s DOM manipulation features, and after going through several tutorials and documentation, I learned to use functions like querySelector and getElementsByTagName to access and update HTML elements dynamically. By experimenting with these methods, I was able to create a modal that displays different project titles, images, and descriptions based on the portfolio card clicked. This allowed me to create a more interactive and user-friendly experience for my site.

The second thing I learned was how to manage multiple event listeners in a clean and efficient way. As I added interactivity to my site, such as opening a modal when a card is clicked or closing it by clicking on various elements, I realised I needed a way to handle multiple event listeners without cluttering my code. I took the time to study event delegation, learning how it could reduce the number of event listeners while still providing the desired functionality. I practised by writing JavaScript functions that responded to various user actions (like clicking the overlay, close buttons, or portfolio cards). With the help of online resources and some trial and error, I became comfortable working with events and creating modular, scalable code.

The third challenge I faced was mastering CSS class manipulation to manage UI states. I wanted to control when elements like modals or overlays would appear and disappear smoothly. To achieve this, I learned to dynamically add and remove CSS classes using JavaScript. I read articles and watched tutorials that explained how combining CSS animations with JavaScript could enhance the user experience. After multiple iterations, I managed to create a seamless modal opening and closing effect by toggling the “active” class based on user actions. This not only improved the visual aesthetics of the site but also made the code more maintainable.

Evaluation I: What aspects of your work do you think were particularly successful? Why?

I am particularly proud of the parallax scrolling effect I implemented on the main page of my website. This feature stands out as a success for several reasons. Firstly, the motion dynamics of the image objects, which move in response to the user’s scrolling, are smooth and consistent across all viewport sizes. This responsiveness ensures a seamless experience regardless of the device used, which was one of my main goals.

At first, the idea of implementing parallax scrolling felt quite daunting. Most of the tutorials I found used preset images with backgrounds removed to layer the images effectively and create the desired depth. The complexity shown in these tutorials made me apprehensive about whether I could replicate such an effect successfully.

However, as I followed a step-by-step YouTube tutorial, I realised that the process was more approachable than I had anticipated. Encouraged by this, I went beyond the basic vertical movements suggested in the tutorial, incorporating rotations and horizontal shifts of the image objects to add complexity and enhance the appeal of the effect.

In the end, I customised the parallax scrolling to reflect personal elements, choosing images that resonate with my identity as a proud Singaporean and modifying them to fit the theme. The success of this feature lies not only in its technical execution but also in how it personalises the user experience, maintaining a thematic consistency that aligns with the overall design ethos of the site. The positive feedback I received confirmed its impact, making it one of the highlights of my web development project.

Evaluation II: What aspects of your work could be improved? How might you do things differently another time?

One aspect of my work that I believe could be improved is the pop-up effect on my portfolio page. Inspired by the sleek and minimalistic pop-ups found on the Apple website, I aimed to replicate their clean design and intuitive user experience, particularly the way they handle overflowing content. On Apple’s site, when the content within a pop-up exceeds the available space, users can scroll down, and the entire pop-up moves smoothly to reveal more information.

However, in my implementation, I was unable to achieve the same effect. Instead of the pop-up moving in response to scrolling, I opted for a simpler solution where the content within the pop-up is scrollable within a fixed frame. While this approach works functionally and maintains a clean design, I feel it lacks the fluidity and polish of Apple’s interaction model, where the entire pop-up moves as the user scrolls, creating a more seamless experience.

The main challenge I faced was managing content overflow. Although the scrollable pop-up I created is a practical solution, it doesn’t quite match the refined user experience I had envisioned. In the future, I plan to research and experiment with advanced JavaScript techniques, such as manipulating the viewport’s scroll event or using CSS animations, to create a more dynamic pop-up movement.

Given more time or another opportunity, I would focus on learning how to implement this type of scrollable, moving pop-up. This might involve a deeper exploration of JavaScript event listeners or CSS properties like position: sticky and transform, which could help me achieve an effect similar to what Apple has done. By refining this feature, I can bring my design closer to the high standard of interactivity and user experience that I admire in professional websites.
